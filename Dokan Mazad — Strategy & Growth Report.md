## Who This Report Is For

This report is written for the Dokan Mazad team. It covers the market, the buyer, the competitors, the actions we need to take in sequence, and the content we should be producing. It is designed to be read by someone who wants to know exactly what to do and why — not by someone looking for a theoretical overview.

Dokan Mazad imports pre-owned Korean vehicles and delivers them end-to-end to buyers across Saudi Arabia, UAE, Jordan, and Iraq *(geography to be confirmed — demand signals from Egypt, Algeria, and broader MENA also present)*. The buyer pays once. Dokan Mazad handles everything from auction sourcing in Korea to customs clearance and delivery. The Korean side is invisible to the client.

This report treats that model as the starting point and builds a growth strategy around it.

## The Market in Numbers

The Middle East used car market is valued at **$93 billion in 2025** and is forecast to reach **$120 billion by 2030**, growing at 5.29% annually *(Mordor Intelligence, 2025)*. Saudi Arabia's used car market alone sits at approximately **$10 billion in 2025**, expanding at an 8.58% CAGR through 2031 *(Mordor Intelligence, 2025)*. The UAE used car market is valued at **$20.6 billion in 2025** and is projected to reach **$35.8 billion by 2030** — an 11.73% CAGR, one of the fastest growth rates in the region *(Mordor Intelligence, 2025)*. In Saudi Arabia, used vehicles account for **60% of total car sales in the country** — the majority of the automotive market is not new vehicles.

On the supply side, South Korea exported **$8.9 billion worth of used vehicles in 2025**, up 75% year-over-year *(Reuters / Glottis Global, 2025)*. The Middle East accounts for roughly 30% of those exports. Korean vehicles hold a structural advantage over Japanese imports across MENA: Korea drives on the right, Japan drives on the left — in markets where left-hand-drive is required, Korean cars are the only viable Asian import option.

There is, however, a real headwind. Korean used car exports to the Middle East declined in 2025 due to Red Sea shipping disruptions and regional geopolitical tensions — Korea is now actively seeking alternative export markets to compensate *(Korea Times, 2026)*. This is not an abstract risk for Dokan Mazad. It is a supply chain and logistics variable that sits underneath the entire business model.

The buyer fueling demand growth in this market is not someone who set out to import a car from Korea. He is someone who looked at the price gap between a Korean import and a local dealer — 40 to 60 percent — and started asking who can make that happen for him.

## The One Buyer We Are Targeting

Every decision in this report — what to post, where to post it, what to say, what to prove — is made for one specific person.

He is in his **late twenties to mid-thirties**. He is male. He lives in Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Jordan, Iraq, Kuwait, Qatar, or Oman. He is not wealthy — over 90% of buyers in this market cite an insufficient budget as their primary barrier to car ownership. He does not need a trigger event to want a car. He has always wanted one. The Korean import model is the first time the car he actually wants has felt within reach.

This is the primary audience: young adults who are aspirational but not yet actively searching, price-aware but not yet problem-aware. They are not researching solutions — they are dreaming. The content that reaches them is built around desire, not necessity. A real car, the one he actually wants, at a price that does not require him to compromise.

Along the way, that same content reaches the buyer whose life has changed — the one who just got married, whose first child shifted the calculation, whose new job finally justified the spend. Those buyers exist and they convert well. But designing the content around them narrows the reach. The larger pool is people who are watching, dreaming, and sharing long before they have any practical reason to act. That is who the content is for. The others will find it and recognize themselves in it.

He is not a car enthusiast. He is not researching gear ratios or suspension specs. He found out — through TikTok, through a friend, through a YouTube video he almost scrolled past — that the same car sitting on a local dealer's lot for 60,000 riyals can be sourced directly from Korea for 35,000. He did the math. He is now asking one question: **how do I actually do this, and who can I trust to do it for me?**

That question is the entire business.

---

**What this transaction actually feels like from his side**

He is being asked to wire a significant sum of money — often between $8,000 and $25,000 — to a company in a country he has never visited, for a car he cannot touch, inspect, or sit in. Once the money leaves his account, the recovery rate for wire fraud is below 15%, even with immediate action *(SH Global Auto, 2025)*. He knows, or senses, that the moment he transfers the funds he has surrendered all leverage. He is now dependent entirely on the integrity of someone he found online.

He has read the stories. A buyer paid for two cars and received nothing after five months. Another received a car with an odometer showing 60,000 kilometers — confirmed electronically at over 250,000. Another got a vehicle described as unrepaired, which arrived with mismatched body panels, repainted sections, and interior splattered with workshop overspray. One customer requested a refund and was told there would be penalties, with no explanation of how those penalties were calculated *(Autowini Trustpilot reviews, 2025)*.

Real buyers on r/saudicars describe odometer fraud with a phrase that has become shorthand for the entire category: *"الغش فيها مثل السلام عليكم"* — the fraud in them is like saying hello. It is so common it is the default assumption, not a risk to be managed.

This is the emotional state he is in when he considers making a purchase. Not excitement. Not confidence. **A desire that is larger than his fear, but only just.**

---

**What he cannot verify and what that costs him psychologically**

He cannot inspect the car before paying. He cannot visit Korea. He cannot read the Korean auction records without help. He cannot confirm that the inspection certificate is real — scammers produce professional-looking fake KAIDA membership certificates and fabricated business registration documents visually indistinguishable from genuine ones *(SH Global Auto, 2025)*.

Buyers who do their own research have developed a verification toolkit: running VINs through Google to find auction photos, using carhistory.or.kr (a Korean government inspection site, $1 per check), requesting CarSeer reports, and trusting Encar above all other platforms — *"موقع Encar هو الأضمن، أما المواقع الثانية انتبهوا منها"* ("Encar is the safest, be careful of other websites"). But even Encar is not immune: one buyer reported receiving a car with five repainted parts when the Encar inspection report showed only two. The trust gap goes deeper than the verification tools can close.

He also does not know what the car will actually cost him. The price quoted is the FOB price. What he does not yet know is that customs duty in Saudi Arabia runs 5–15%, VAT is 15%, sea freight adds 6,500–9,200 SAR, port handling adds more, registration adds more, and if his documentation is wrong he faces demurrage charges for every day the car sits waiting for clearance. The real landed cost typically runs **19–35% above the FOB price** *(SH Global Auto, 2025; iCartea, 2025)*. Most buyers budget only for the car. Many only discover the gap exists when they are asked to pay it.

Further confusion comes from the two purchase models that exist in the market — the customs card method, where the total landed price is quoted upfront, and the auction method, where the buyer pays the base price and discovers the additional costs later. Buyers in Arabic forums are visibly confused about which model applies to them, and the distinction is rarely explained before money changes hands.

Then there is the 25–45 day shipping window. He has paid. He has nothing to show for it. There are no updates unless he asks. Every day of silence feels, to some part of him, like the beginning of a scam.

And when the car arrives — if everything went correctly — he still faces SASO compliance certification, customs clearance, a Traffic Department technical inspection, insurance, and registration. He is alone in a bureaucratic process he did not know existed when he started.

---

**The specific fears that go beyond the general**

Beyond odometer fraud and hidden costs, four fears surfaced repeatedly in Arabic buyer forums that do not appear in industry research:

**ECU lock and software incompatibility.** *"السوفتوير مختلف وعليه lock، راح تتعبك في البرمجة"* — the software is different and locked, it will cause you problems in programming. Korean domestic vehicles ship with ECU software calibrated to Korean specifications. When a sensor fails or a radar module needs replacing, the car must be reprogrammed to accept the part. Specialists who can do this are rare and expensive across the Gulf. For German brands imported through Korea — Mercedes, BMW — the problem is compounded: the Korean ECU variant is incompatible with American, Gulf, and European spec parts. Buyers who investigated this consulted multiple experts and came back with the same answer. The car is not fixable through normal channels.

**Korean-spec AC compressors.** Multiple buyers across r/saudicars threads raised the same concern: *"نظام التكييف الكوري أضعف"* — the Korean AC system is weaker. Korean domestic vehicles are built for a temperate climate. The compressor is smaller. In Saudi Arabia and the UAE, where AC runs constantly at full capacity for six months of the year, this is not a comfort issue — it is a reliability issue. Buyers who raised it were not dismissed. They were validated by other buyers who had experienced it.

**The screen arrives in Korean.** *"شاشة سيارتي كانت باللغة الكورية ولا تدعم الإنجليزية"* — my car's screen was in Korean and did not support English. This is a post-delivery surprise that no seller warned him about before purchase. The infotainment system on Korean domestic vehicles is often region-locked with no Arabic or English language option. It is not a dealbreaker — but it is a daily reminder that the car was built for someone else, and it surfaces in public every time someone sits in his passenger seat.

**Taxis and flood-damaged cars.** *"التاكسي والغرقان يجون من كوريا كثير"* — taxis and flooded cars come from Korea a lot. This is a specific, documented buyer belief: that a meaningful portion of Korean used car exports are retired commercial vehicles and flood-damaged cars with rolled-back odometers. Whether statistically accurate or not, the belief is widespread enough that it shapes how buyers evaluate any Korean import they cannot physically inspect.

---

**He is not evaluating a product. He is trying to eliminate regret.**

The clearest window into this buyer's psychology is not a survey or a demographic report. It is the questions he asks in public, on a stranger's TikTok video, before he has committed to anything.

In a single video about self-importing a Korean car — 30,400 likes, 20,100 shares — six questions appeared in the comments. Each one looks like a practical question. Each one is a fear.

**"Can you do maintenance at the dealership? Some Hyundais have chassis numbers that don't register in the diagnostic system."**
The fear: I will be locked out of official servicing forever. No dealer will be able to scan my car. I will own something no one can properly fix.

**"Will the specs be Gulf spec? Do I need to replace the radiator and fans?"**
The fear: The car will break down in Saudi heat. It was not built for this climate. I will spend money fixing something that should have worked from the first day.

**"The difference is 20-30k but after shipping, customs, clearance and VAT it disappears — where is the actual saving?"**
The fear: I am going to go through all this complexity and end up paying the same as buying locally. The whole thing is an illusion. I will look like an idiot.

**"Does it come with a spare tire or without?"**
The fear: There are details and missing items I do not know about. The car will arrive incomplete. Hidden gaps will cost me money I did not budget for.

**"How much is the actual price difference — or is it just for the sake of saying I imported from Korea?"**
The fear: The savings might not be real. Am I doing this for financial benefit or for social validation? Is this just expensive bragging rights?

**"Are spare parts available in Saudi Arabia?"**
The fear: When something breaks — and something always breaks — I will not be able to fix it. I will own a car I cannot maintain in my own country.

He asks all six before committing. He is not looking for excitement. He is looking for the thing that will go wrong — so he does not have to live with it afterward. This is a buyer who has already decided he wants to do this, and is now building a case against himself to make sure he is not making a mistake.

The share count on that video — 20,100 — tells you something else. He is not keeping these questions to himself. He is sending the video to someone. A friend. A brother. Someone whose opinion matters before he pulls the trigger. The decision is social even when it feels private.

---

**What makes him different from a buyer in any other market**

The Western buyer importing a car from Korea has forums, Reddit threads with step-by-step documentation, YouTube creators in his language, and a community of experienced buyers he can reach before making a decision.

This buyer has a community. It exists — on r/saudicars, in TikTok comment sections, on WhatsApp groups, in Arabic automotive forums. People are asking the same questions he is asking, and some of them have done it. What is missing is not community. It is reliability. There is no trusted Arabic-language source of record for this process. Threads go unanswered or are answered by sellers. Content that exists in Arabic skews promotional. WhatsApp groups are invite-only with inconsistent information. The Western buyer's advantage is not access to community — it is access to a community where experienced buyers answer questions from people who can't pay them.

The pattern across every Arabic thread is not silence. It is unresolved demand. A buyer posts a question. The replies are overwhelmingly *"مهتم"* (interested) and *"علقت للعودة للبوست"* (commented to come back to this post). These are buyers who have the desire, cannot find a trusted answer, and are waiting for someone they trust to go first.

He does not ask *"where can I find information."* He knows where to look. What he asks is: **مين جرب؟** — *who has tried it?* He is not searching for a forum. He is searching for a person he trusts who has already done this and is willing to prove it.

Peer validation from someone who visibly completed the process — not the website, not the price list, not the inspection report — is the single most powerful force in his decision.

The reliability gap also points to a specific content type that no Arabic importer has built and that does not require a buyer to produce it. A seller-made A-to-Z process video: not a promotion, not a testimonial compilation, but a documented walkthrough of exactly what happens between payment and delivery — narrated by the people doing the work, showing the auction, the inspection, the container, the customs, the handover. The buyer cannot find a trustworthy peer who filmed the whole journey. But he will accept the next best thing: a seller transparent enough to show him every step before he commits. That single content type does more to close the trust gap than any testimonial reel, because it answers the question underneath the question — *not just did it work for someone else, but do I understand exactly what I am agreeing to.*

> **Content type to build: The Full Process Video (A to Z)** — seller-produced, unscripted, documentary style. Every step from auction bid to delivery handover. No cuts that skip the uncomfortable parts. This is the content that converts the informed, skeptical buyer who has done his research and is still on the fence.

---

**The gap this creates**

He is a buyer with real desire, real money, and a willingness to act — held back not by disinterest but by a trust gap that no one has adequately closed for him in Arabic, at scale, with proof.

Whoever builds the content, the process transparency, and the social proof infrastructure that this buyer is currently looking for and not finding will own his decision before he has even started comparing prices.

That is the position Dokan Mazad exists to fill.

## How the Trigger Works

The buyer does not decide to import a car from Korea. He is shown that it is possible, and then the idea takes hold.

Understanding how that happens — the exact sequence from first exposure to first contact — determines where Dokan Mazad needs to show up, what it needs to say at each point, and why the current content strategy misses most of the journey entirely.

---

**Stage 1 — Exposure: The scroll that changes the math**

It starts on TikTok. He is not searching for anything. He is not in market. He scrolls past a video — someone showing a car they imported from Korea, or a price comparison, or a creator walking through what they paid versus what the dealer was asking. The number stops him. 60,000 at the dealer. 35,000 from Korea. He watches the whole video.

This is not a decision moment. It is a math moment. The idea that the car he wants — not a compromise, the actual car — might be within reach lands in his head and does not leave. He may not act for weeks or months. But something has shifted.

This stage is almost never triggered by a search. It is triggered by a scroll. Which means a brand that is not on TikTok producing content that creates this moment does not exist at Stage 1. The trigger fires without them, or it does not fire at all.

---

**Stage 2 — The informal calculation**

He does not open Google. He does rough math in his head, or on his phone calculator, or with a friend. He tries to figure out if the numbers are real. He has seen one video. He does not yet know about customs duty, VAT, shipping, clearance, or registration. He is calculating off the FOB price because that is the only number he has seen.

At this stage, the gap feels enormous and the idea feels viable. The fears have not surfaced yet. He is still in the possibility. The content that should exist here — a simple, honest "here is what it actually costs, total, with everything included" — does not exist in Arabic at scale. He cannot find a reliable number. So he holds the idea loosely and keeps scrolling.

---

**Stage 3 — Social validation: مين جرب؟**

Before he researches anything formally, he shares. He sends the video to a friend. A brother. Someone whose opinion he trusts before he trusts his own. He is not asking for permission — he is stress-testing the idea against someone he respects.

The 20,100 shares on a single TikTok video about Korean car imports is not an anomaly. It is the mechanism. Sharing is how this buyer validates before committing. Every share is a person sending the video to their social circle and asking, implicitly, *مين جرب؟* — who has tried this?

If the answer comes back with someone who did it and it worked, the trigger fires and he moves to research. If no one in his circle has done it, he stays here — interested, watching, waiting for someone he trusts to go first. This is the stage where most buyers stall. Not because they lost interest. Because they could not find the social proof they needed to feel safe moving forward.

---

**Stage 4 — Active research: building the case**

Once someone he trusts validates the idea — or a piece of content convinces him enough to investigate — he starts searching. He finds Reddit threads on r/saudicars. He reads TikTok comments. He looks up importers. He asks questions publicly. He bookmarks posts to come back to. He discovers the fears from Section 3: the odometer fraud, the hidden costs, the AC compressor, the ECU lock, the screen in Korean.

This stage takes time. The average buyer spends approximately three months between first consideration and purchase. He is not slow — he is thorough. He is building a case against himself, as described in Section 3, trying to find every reason this could go wrong before he commits.

The content that closes this stage is not promotional. It is proof. The inspection video. The cost breakdown. The process walkthrough. The A-to-Z documentary. These are the pieces that answer the fears one by one and make the last remaining barrier — the wire transfer — feel survivable.

---

**Stage 5 — The decision: contact**

He sends a WhatsApp message. This is the last step in a journey that started with a scroll, moved through informal math, social validation, and three months of research. By the time he contacts an importer, he has made most of the decision already. What he is looking for in that first exchange is confirmation that the person on the other end is real, responsive, and capable of handling what they have promised.

The importer who responds fastest, most clearly, and most humanly to that first message wins a disproportionate number of deals — not because they have the best price, but because the buyer's anxiety peaks at contact and the first response either relieves it or compounds it.

---

**Where Dokan Mazad currently shows up in this sequence**

| Stage | What the buyer needs | Dokan Mazad's presence |
|-------|---------------------|------------------------|
| 1 — Exposure | Content that creates the price discovery moment | Absent |
| 2 — Informal calculation | Honest total cost breakdown | Absent |
| 3 — Social validation | Peer proof, shareable content, مين جرب؟ answered | Partially — delivery videos exist but are not optimized for sharing |
| 4 — Active research | Process transparency, fear-answering content, inspection proof | Absent |
| 5 — Contact | Fast, human, trust-building first response | Unknown — no data on response quality or speed |

They are present at Stage 3 in a limited way. They are absent from every other stage. The buyer who has not yet heard of Korean car imports — the primary audience this report is built around — will never encounter Dokan Mazad until after someone else has already created the trigger moment for them.

## The Competitive Landscape

The competitive picture for Dokan Mazad is better than it looks on paper and worse than it looks in the mirror.

Better than it looks on paper: there is no dominant Arabic-native Korean car import brand in the MENA market. The space is wide open. Whoever builds the content infrastructure, the trust signals, and the brand authority first will own the buyer's decision before anyone else is even in the conversation.

Worse than it looks in the mirror: Dokan Mazad is the most obvious candidate to own that position and is currently not claiming it. Every problem documented in Section 6 is a gap that a competitor could step into.

---

**The direct service competitors**

**Autowini** is the largest Korean used car export platform in the world. Founded in 2010, it operates as a marketplace connecting Korean dealers with international buyers across dozens of countries. It has Arabic language support but is English-first by nature and global by design — it is not built for the Arab buyer the way an Arabic-native brand would be. Its scale is its strength and its weakness: massive inventory and global credibility, but no cultural proximity, no Arabic content strategy, no presence on TikTok in Arabic, and no end-to-end managed service. The buyer who uses Autowini is largely doing the import himself. The buyer Dokan Mazad is targeting wants someone to handle everything for him. These are different buyers, or the same buyer at different levels of confidence.

**SH Global Auto** has Arabic language support and KAIDA membership — a visible trust credential Dokan Mazad does not display publicly. But SH Global operates primarily in Central Asia (Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan) and Africa (Nigeria, Kenya), with a luxury-focused inventory priced between $40,000 and $70,000 FOB. Genesis G90s. Mercedes GLE. Porsche. This is not the buyer this report is built around. SH Global is a premium export house serving a different segment in a different geography. They are not competing for the same customer.

**Local Korean import dealers** — businesses in Saudi Arabia, UAE, and Jordan that import from Korea and sell locally — are the only competitors with physical presence. A buyer can visit, inspect, and sit in the car before buying. That is a real advantage in a market where the primary fear is buying something unseen. The cost of that advantage is the 40–60% premium over direct import prices. They are competing on trust, not price. Dokan Mazad competes on price and must win on trust through content rather than presence.

---

**What research revealed about listed competitors**

Two names that appear in early research on this market do not hold up to verification.

**DUMSAN** is not a competitor. dumsan.com carries the footer "Made with Love ❤️ by © Dokan Mazad 2026" and runs on the identical Next.js codebase — confirmed by matching CSS fingerprints. DUMSAN is a Dokan Mazad B2B platform customer operating their own branded export business on top of Dokan Mazad's infrastructure. They are a client, not competition.

**Korabia** is not a Korean car import service. korabia.com is an Egyptian sports news portal. The name appeared in early market research and was carried forward incorrectly.

---

**The real competition: content creators and inertia**

The most significant competition Dokan Mazad faces is not another importer. It is two things: Arabic-language TikTok creators who are educating the buyer about Korean car imports without offering a service, and the buyer's own inertia at Stage 3 of the trigger model.

The active TikTok accounts in this space, confirmed by username:

- **@alhamadksa** (Alhamad Cars KSA) — a local Korean import dealer with an active TikTok presence. We have direct comment data from their content: buyers asking about inspection guarantees, spare parts availability, and software lock issues — all unanswered publicly. They are generating real buyer engagement and leaving questions on the table.

- **@kafoart1** (كفو آرت لإستيراد السيارات — Kafoo Art for Car Importing) — an Arabic-language Korean car import service with its own TikTok presence. A direct content competitor in the same language and targeting the same buyer.

- **@korcar** (Kor Car) — another active player in the Korean car import space on TikTok.

- **@kafoocar** (KafooCar) — a separate business using Dokan Mazad's B2B platform, confirmed by kafoocar.dokanmazad.com in SSL certificate records. An independent consumer-facing Korean car import brand with its own TikTok presence, running on Dokan Mazad's infrastructure.

- **@hallakorea** (HALLAKOREA) — a separate business using Dokan Mazad's B2B platform, confirmed by hallakorea.dokanmazad.com in SSL certificate records. Another independent consumer-facing brand built on the same infrastructure.

These two are not internal operators — they are B2B platform customers who have built their own consumer brands and their own TikTok audiences using the infrastructure Dokan Mazad provides. Having businesses like this as platform customers is not a problem — it is a sign the platform works. The execution gap is that they currently live under dokanmazad.com subdomains instead of their own independent brand identity. The fix is simple: platform customers operate under their own brand, not under Dokan Mazad's domain. Once that separation is enforced, these businesses strengthen the ecosystem rather than blur it.

The TikTok creators across this space are doing the top-of-funnel work that converts an unaware buyer into someone actively looking for a service. They explain why Korea, what the process looks like, what the risks are. They are handing warm, educated prospects to whichever importer those buyers find next. Right now Dokan Mazad is not reliably that importer, because it is absent from the content layer where the creators are operating.

The buyer's inertia is the other competitor. A buyer who reaches Stage 3 — socially validated, interested, but unable to find a trusted peer who completed the process — does not usually go to a competitor. He waits. He bookmarks. He comments *علقت للعودة للبوست* and comes back six months later. He is not lost to a competitor. He is suspended in uncertainty, and whoever breaks that uncertainty first with credible, human, Arabic content wins him.

---

**The competitive position Dokan Mazad can own**

No Arabic-native brand has built the content infrastructure, the process transparency, or the social proof system that the buyer in Section 3 is looking for. Autowini is too global. SH Global is too premium. Local dealers are too expensive. The TikTok creators are not selling anything.

The position — Arabic-first, end-to-end managed import service, transparent process, human brand — is unoccupied. It will not stay unoccupied indefinitely. The market is growing at double digits. The content gap is visible to anyone paying attention. The question is whether Dokan Mazad claims it first or watches someone else build it.

## What Exists Now and Why It Is Not Working

Dokan Mazad has the right product in the right market at the right time. What it does not have is a presence that matches any of that. The gap between the opportunity and how the brand currently shows up is the central problem this report addresses.

---

**The website**

The main site at dokanmazad.com has four problems that compound each other.

The first is framing. The "Who We Are" section is written as if the company needs to introduce itself. It does not. The buyer does not care who Dokan Mazad is — he cares what Dokan Mazad will do for him, and what happens to his money. "Who we are" answers the wrong question.

The second is a feature that should not exist. The site has a "My Orders" section, the kind of interface element that belongs in an e-commerce checkout flow. This is not an online shop. The buyer is not ordering a product off a shelf. He is commissioning a managed service involving $10,000 to $25,000, international logistics, and Korean auction expertise. An "My Orders" panel makes the whole operation look like a side project someone built on a Shopify template. It damages credibility at the exact moment the site should be building it.

The third is the "How It Works" page — the single most important page on the site for a buyer in the consideration phase. It does not always load correctly. When it does load, delivery timelines are stated as fixed dates rather than honest windows. The CTAs on this page are not designed to move anyone. A buyer who arrives on this page wanting to understand the process well enough to commit is not being served.

The fourth is brand inconsistency. The landing page does not hold to a consistent visual or tonal identity. The CTAs across the site are weak — they do not create urgency, they do not speak to the buyer's emotional state, and they do not make the next step feel safe. A buyer who arrived because a video made him feel something will feel nothing when he hits the site.

There is also no pricing proof. The site claims transparent pricing. It does not show it. A total cost calculator — car price, shipping, customs duty, VAT, SASO certification, registration, broken down by destination country — does not exist. The buyer who needs to know whether the savings are real before he commits cannot find that answer on the site. He leaves.

---

**The social media presence**

Dokan Mazad operates five separate Instagram accounts under five different usernames with no coordination between them:

- **`dokanmazad`** — the primary account. 139 posts, 5,961 followers. This is the real page.
- **`dokan.mazad`** — operated by Mubarak. 233 posts, 587 followers. More posts than the main account, a fraction of the following.
- **`dokanmazad_`** — operated by Rayan. 3 posts, 642 followers. Links to 102.dokanmazad.com.
- **`dokan_mazad`** — 4 posts, 2 followers.
- **`dm_as63`** — operated by Ali Saeed. 3 posts, 11 followers.

Five accounts. Three of them have fewer followers than a personal account. One of them has more posts than the official brand page and a fraction of its audience. Every one of them is operating with different copy, different tone, different visual standards, and a different phone number. A buyer who encounters any of them other than the main account gets a version of Dokan Mazad that looks informal, incomplete, and unverifiable as legitimate. In a fraud-aware market, this fragmentation does not read as a growing brand with multiple team members. It reads as the kind of operation that scams people.

**YouTube:** One video. Functionally absent. This is worse than having no channel in some ways — it signals they tried and gave up. A buyer who checks YouTube as part of their due diligence finds a dead channel and draws their own conclusions.

**Snapchat:** No official Dokan Mazad account exists. What exists is "Mubarak Dukkan Mazad" — an individual running his own account, not the brand. Snapchat is the platform where their exact demographic is most active in Saudi Arabia. The brand is completely absent on the single platform most suited to reaching them.

The same fragmentation exists at the domain level, and it is more extensive than it appears. SSL certificate records reveal 30 active operator subdomains under dokanmazad.com, confirmed by Let's Encrypt and Google Trust Services certificates logged between mid-2024 and April 2026.

The brand's biggest structural advantage — direct access to Korean auctions, the ability to navigate a system that is genuinely opaque to the buyer — is invisible in every piece of content that currently exists.

## The Action Plan

Dokan Mazad operates a B2B platform alongside its consumer service. Independent businesses — including @kafoocar and @hallakorea — have signed up, built their own brands, and are active in the same consumer market using Dokan Mazad's infrastructure. This is not a problem with the model. It is a problem with one thing: those businesses currently live under dokanmazad.com subdomains. To the buyer, they look like Dokan Mazad. To Google, they fragment the brand. To anyone doing due diligence, they create doubt.

The decision required is simple: platform customers must build under their own brand identity, not under dokanmazad.com. This means new platform customers get their own domain from day one. Existing customers on subdomains are migrated. The builder tool continues to exist — it just no longer produces pages that carry the Dokan Mazad name and domain.

Not making this decision has a direct cost. Every piece of content, every trust signal, and every SAR spent on brand building in the plan below is partially undermined as long as 30 versions of the brand exist in public. The brand is being built with one hand and leaked with the other. The decision is a single conversation. The cost of not having it compounds every month.

That conversation needs to happen. The rest of this plan moves forward regardless of when it does.

**Phase 1 — Fix and Consolidate**

Phase 1 requires no content creation and no new strategy. It is the work that eliminates what is actively costing money and clears the ground for everything that follows.

**Close the gaps costing money today.** Three things are destroying leads right now. The expired SSL certificates on agent subdomains — abufahad.dokanmazad.com, hallakorea.dokanmazad.com, ezcars.dokanmazad.com — are serving browser security warnings to buyers following referral links. The primary accounts — **@dokanmazad** on Instagram and **@dokanmazad** on TikTok — are the only ones that remain publicly active. The others are wound down or redirected. Every follower and every piece of engagement that currently feeds secondary accounts starts feeding the primary. Splitting the audience across five accounts means building five small things instead of one meaningful one.

Not a spokesperson, not a host — someone who works at Dokan Mazad, knows the process, and can explain it in a way that makes the buyer feel like they are dealing with a person who will personally make sure things go right.

**Phase 2 — Build the Content That Does Not Exist**

Phase 2 is where the brand goes from present to visible. The content built here does not exist in Arabic at scale from any importer in this market. Building it is the strategic opportunity.

**The "why Korea" content.** The buyer who has never heard of Korean car importing needs to understand the price gap, the spec advantage, and why Korea specifically before he can consider Dokan Mazad. This content lives at Stage 1 of the trigger model — TikTok, short-form, designed to be shared. It is not promotional. It is educational. It creates the moment of price discovery that starts the entire buyer journey. Without it, Dokan Mazad is invisible to every unaware buyer.

**The full cost breakdown.** One piece of content, one country at a time. Saudi Arabia first — every riyal from FOB price to registration. Car price. Shipping. Customs duty. VAT. SASO certification. Port handling. Registration. Total. No ambiguity, no estimates, no ranges. The buyer who watches this video knows exactly what his money buys before he has spoken to anyone. This answers the fear of hidden costs and eliminates the most common reason buyers stall. A static version of this breakdown also lives on the website — the total cost calculator the site currently claims to offer but does not have.

**The A-to-Z process video.** A single documentary-style video — seller-produced, unscripted — showing exactly what happens between payment and delivery. The auction floor. The pre-shipment inspection. The container loading. The port. The customs clearance. The delivery handover. Every step. No cuts that skip the uncomfortable parts. This video does not need to be polished. It needs to be real. A buyer who watches it understands what he is agreeing to before he transfers a single riyal.

**The rejection video.** Dokan Mazad inspects cars before buying them. Some are rejected. Showing a car being rejected — and explaining exactly why — proves quality control is real in a way no written guarantee can. This directly addresses the fears of odometer fraud, taxi history, and flood damage without making claims. The proof is in the action.

**Delivery content with context.** The delivery videos that already exist are the right format in the wrong frame. Instead of "car arrived, customer happy," the frame becomes: this is Hassan, this is the car he chose, this is what it cost him in total, this is what he thought of the process. Name, car, real total cost, honest assessment. That content answers مين جرب؟ in a way that a handover photo never can.

**AI-looking content stops.** Every video in Phase 2 and beyond feels like a person made it for a person. This is not optional — in a market where the primary buyer fear is fraud, synthetic-looking content is not a production quality issue. It is a trust destruction issue.

**Phase 3 — Grow and Scale**

Phase 3 takes what Phase 2 built and turns it into a compounding system. The content exists. The face is established. The identity is consolidated. Now the goal is reach, consistency, and proof at scale.

**Grow @dokanmazad on TikTok.** The existing account is the asset — not a starting point to abandon. Consistent, human, Arabic-first content across four content types: awareness content that creates the price discovery moment, process content that demystifies the journey, fear-answering content delivered on camera by the assigned face, and proof content framed as full customer stories. The account grows because the content earns it, not because the algorithm is gamed.

**Launch Snapchat.** Dokan Mazad has no official Snapchat account. An official account is created and maintained with the same face and content direction as TikTok. The Saudi demographic this brand is built around is more active on Snapchat than any other platform. Absence there is not neutral.

**Build the social proof infrastructure.** Social proof is not a content type — it is a system that runs permanently. Every completed transaction triggers a review request across Haraj, Google, and Instagram. Every satisfied buyer is asked on camera. Every WhatsApp message of praise becomes a content asset with permission. Buyers who received their car three to six months ago are followed up and asked to speak on camera — they are the most credible voice in the market and the most underused asset Dokan Mazad has. UGC from buyers who post their own delivery videos is collected, reposted, and celebrated. The answer to مين جرب؟ becomes unavoidable wherever the next buyer is looking.

**The Campaign Strategy**

The campaign is organic and ongoing. There is no hard launch date — the strategy builds progressively as the phases complete. Content compounds. Each piece does its job in the buyer journey, and the library grows.

All content posts on both TikTok and Instagram. The same video that reaches a buyer scrolling TikTok at Stage 1 is also on Instagram where a buyer at Stage 3 is looking for proof. The platforms reach the same person at different moments in his journey — the content does not change between them.

**The Content Series**

The campaign runs through five ongoing content series, each mapped to a specific job in the buyer journey. Series run in parallel, not in sequence.

**ليش كوريا؟ — Why Korea?**
*Stage 1 — Awareness*
Short-form TikTok content designed to stop the scroll and create the price discovery moment for a buyer who has never considered importing. The format is simple: the gap between what the dealer charges and what it actually costs through Dokan Mazad, made real with specific cars and specific numbers. This series is the entry point for every buyer who does not yet know the option exists. It should be the most-shared content Dokan Mazad produces.

**بكم وقفت عليك؟ — What Did It Cost You?**
*Stage 3 — Social Validation*
One buyer. One car. The total landed cost — every riyal. His honest assessment of the process. This is the مين جرب؟ answer on repeat. Each episode is a complete story that a potential buyer can send to his brother and say "watch this." The name, the car, the number, the experience. No promotional framing. Just a person who did it, talking about what it was like. This series runs on both TikTok and Instagram.

**شاهد قبل ما تحول — Watch Before You Transfer**
*Stage 4 — Active Research*
The process, broken into episodes. Each episode covers one step of the journey — the auction, the inspection, the shipping, the customs clearance, the delivery. Short enough for TikTok, detailed enough to answer the fear behind each step. This series is what the buyer finds when he searches for information about how the process works. It answers the questions he is asking before he has to ask them.

**رفضناها ليش؟ — Why Did We Reject It?**
*Stage 4 — Trust*
A car Dokan Mazad inspected and did not buy, and why. The specific reasons — odometer discrepancy, accident history, flood indicators, taxi registration, condition not matching the listing. This series does more to prove quality control than any certificate or claim. Seeing rejection is proof of selection. It also directly addresses the fears buyers name most — the taxi cars, the flooded cars, the rolled odometers — without ever making the conversation defensive.

**اسأل دكان مزاد — Ask Dokan Mazad**
*Stages 3 and 4 — Fear Answering*
The assigned face answers real buyer questions from comments and DMs on camera. One question per video. The six questions identified in Section 3 — the chassis number, the Gulf spec AC, the hidden costs, the spare tire, the real savings, the spare parts — are the starting brief. Every new question from a real buyer is potential content. This series turns the comment section into a content engine and makes the buyer feel heard before he has committed to anything.

---

**Content Cadence**

Minimum four videos per week, posted on both TikTok and Instagram. Consistency matters more than volume — the algorithm rewards accounts that show up regularly, and the buyer who encounters Dokan Mazad on one platform should find the same presence on the other. The content mix rotates across the five series so no single type dominates. Stories on Instagram carry the live conversation — polls, questions, direct engagement with buyers in research mode — as a layer on top of the main content feed.

### Shoot Brief 5 — Face-to-Face Delivery Feedback

**Series:** بكم وقفت عليك؟ — What Did It Cost You?
**Stage:** Stage 3 — Social Validation
**Priority:** High. This is the evolved version of every delivery video in the market — and nobody has done the one-week follow-up.

**The brief:**
Two-part structure for each client story.

*Part 1 — Delivery day:* The client receives his car. On camera, with the Dokan Mazad face present. The client is asked three questions: what car is this, what did it actually cost you in total (every riyal, including shipping, customs, everything), and what was the process like. Not a testimonial. A conversation. If the client says the shipping update was slow, leave it in. The buyer watching this is not looking for perfection — he is looking for honesty.

*Part 2 — One-week follow-up:* The client is contacted one week after delivery. A short video is recorded — in person if possible, on WhatsApp video if not. One question: after a week of driving it, what do you think? No prompts about specs. Just: what do you think? This is the content that does not exist anywhere in this market. Every competitor records the handover. No one comes back a week later. That follow-up is the answer to the AC compressor fear, the software fear, the spare parts fear — not in theory, from someone who has now lived with the car for seven days.

**What this accomplishes:**
The buyer's question is *مين جرب؟* — who has tried it? This answers it with someone who tried it, has lived with it for a week, and is speaking without a script. The one-week version is the most credible content format available in this market and the most absent one.
